# Networked & Participatory Music Performance: The Smartphone Wave (2007-2016)

A summary of key research projects in the first wave of smartphone-era networked music performance, with attention to their milieu, intentions, methods, and conclusions.

---

## Pre-Smartphone Precursors

### Golan Levin — *DialTones (A Telesymphony)* (2001)

**Milieu / context:** Perhaps the earliest concert concept using audience mobile phones as the centrepiece of a sustained live performance. Predates the smartphone era entirely.

**Intentions:** To use the audience's mobile phones as a distributed array of loudspeakers, inverting the usual annoyance of phones ringing in a concert hall into the compositional material itself.

**Methods:** A pre-composed piece played by calling up various numbers of members of the audience. Visual projections displayed the spatial patterns of currently sounding telephones. Audience agency was limited — their role was clearly defined and each member understood how they contributed to the performance.

**Conclusions:** Levin's piece was passive yet spatial in nature, "blurring the performer and audience boundary" (Wang et al. 2008). Notable for its clarity of concept — it did not pretend the audience was "expressing" themselves. The phone was a loudspeaker, not an instrument. The architecture was the composition.


### Quintet.net — Georg Hajdu (2000-ongoing)

**Milieu / context:** Hamburg University of Music and Drama. Emerged from late-1990s excitement about the internet as a medium for real-time creative collaboration, in the wake of telematic performance (Pauline Oliveros / Deep Listening, the Hub in the 1980s).

**Intentions:** To create a real-time interactive environment for composing and performing music over the internet. Distributed musicians contributing to a shared score from remote locations.

**Methods:** Client-server architecture with a Max/MSP-based environment. Performers at different network nodes interacted through a shared graphical score.

**Conclusions:** Hajdu is notable for his honesty about latency as a fundamental constraint requiring "the adaptation of a genuine 'Internet' performance style." Where later smartphone-era projects would gloss over network unreliability, Quintet.net acknowledged the network as an active material constraint, not invisible infrastructure.

**Key papers:**
- Hajdu, G. "Quintet.net: An Environment for Composing and Performing Music on the Internet." *Leonardo*, 38(1), 2005, pp. 23-30.


### Interconnected Musical Networks — Gil Weinberg (2005)

**Milieu / context:** Georgia Tech Center for Music Technology. The mid-2000s saw growing interest in computational models for collaborative music-making, driven partly by the success of laptop orchestras (Princeton PLOrk, 2005) and Web 2.0 rhetoric of participatory culture.

**Intentions:** To establish a theoretical framework for networked music. Proposed a taxonomy addressing "goals, motivations, social organisations, architectures, and musical content." Also developed the Beatbugs — handheld percussive instruments for "interdependent group collaboration."

**Methods:** The Beatbugs used custom hardware with a simple interface allowing creation, manipulation, and sharing of rhythmic motifs. When networked, players formed "large-scale collaborative compositions by interdependently sharing and developing each other's motifs." The theoretical paper classified systems by topology, synchronicity, and musical structure.

**Conclusions:** Weinberg's 2005 *Computer Music Journal* paper became a foundational reference. The framework assumed that networked interdependence was inherently generative — it described *how* to connect musicians, not *whether* connection at scale produces meaningful musical expression. The Beatbugs were designed to allow "novices" to participate, prefiguring the democratisation rhetoric that would intensify with the iPhone.

**Key papers:**
- Weinberg, G. "Interconnected Musical Networks: Toward a Theoretical Framework." *Computer Music Journal*, 29(2), 2005, pp. 23-39.

## The iPhone Moment (2007-2009)

### Context: Apple's Messianic Marketing

The iPhone launched in January 2007. Steve Jobs opened: "Every once in a while a revolutionary product comes along that changes everything." The device was immediately dubbed the "Jesus Phone" — coined by Gizmodo editor Brian Lam in 2006, initially as satire, but which exploded across tech culture after the keynote. Campbell and La Pastina (2010) analysed this phenomenon academically, noting that "transcendent redemption has been part of mythology and religion for centuries, but the source of that redemption has relocated to the scientific and technological." They described Apple stores as "brightly-lit secular churches in our air-conditioned malls, attended to by an army of blue-shirted guides offering advice to the laity."

This matters because the research projects that followed were conceived within this atmosphere — the smartphone as a site of limitless possibility, a universal creative instrument already in everyone's pocket. The post-dot-com scepticism about techno-utopianism had been overcome. The App Store launched in July 2008, and within months, researchers were building instruments for it.

**Key reference:**
- Campbell, H.A. and La Pastina, A.C. "How the iPhone Became Divine: New Media, Religion and the Intertextual Circulation of Meaning." *New Media & Society*, 12(7), 2010, pp. 1191-1207.


### Stanford Mobile Phone Orchestra (MoPhO) — Ge Wang, Georg Essl, Henri Penttinen (2007-ongoing)

**Milieu / context:** Founded at Stanford's CCRMA in 2007, first public concert January 11, 2008. CCRMA carries the legacy of Chowning's FM synthesis research and decades of institutional prestige in computer music. MoPhO was a direct extension of the laptop orchestra model (Princeton PLOrk, Stanford SLOrk) into the consumer device.

**Intentions:** To explore the mobile phone as a legitimate musical instrument for ensemble performance. Wang's founding paper — "Do Mobile Phones Dream of Electric Orchestras?" — deliberately echoed Philip K. Dick. The project envisioned "mobile electronic chamber music": phones as intimate sound sources. A key stated goal was that "the combination of technology, aesthetics, and instrument building presents a potentially powerful pedagogical opportunity."

**Methods:** The original ensemble used 16 Nokia N95 phones running Symbian OS, programmed in C++ and Python — not iPhones (the MoPhO predates the App Store). Each phone's onboard speaker provided sound production with no additional amplification, "keeping true to our notion of 'mobile electronic chamber music.'" 8 pieces were premiered at the first concert, ranging from scored compositions to structured and free improvisations. Pieces exploited accelerometers, keypads, microphones, and cameras.

Notable repertoire included:
- *Drone In/Drone Out* (Wang): FM synthesis with accelerometer-controlled spectral richness, creating "a saturating sonic texture that permeates even large performance spaces despite the limited output power of onboard speakers." Players began disguised among the audience, then revealed themselves and surrounded the listeners.
- *TamaG* (Essl): Explored the tension between human control and device control — "the boundary of projecting the humane onto mobile devices and at the same time displays the fact that they are deeply mechanical and artificifical."
- *Chatter* (Wang): Players distributed throughout the audience "in an effort to immerse the audience in a sonic web of cell phone conversational clouds."

**Conclusions:** Wang concluded that "the technology is stable enough that one can start forming both a well-defined ensemble, and create a persistent repertoire" and looked forward "excitedly" to "diverse developments of this new emerging medium globally." Notably, MoPhO was an *ensemble of trained performers* — not a participatory/mass project. The interesting pieces (*Drone In/Drone Out*, *TamaG*) worked precisely because they were composed and conducted, with performers exploring texture and spatiality rather than individual expression. The rhetorical framing, however, emphasised the phone's ubiquity and accessibility — "they transcend nearly every cultural and economic barrier" — language that elides the reality of a CCRMA graduate ensemble.

**Key papers:**
- Wang, G., Essl, G. and Penttinen, H. "Do Mobile Phones Dream of Electric Orchestras?" *ICMC*, 2008, pp. 331-337.
- Oh, J. et al. "Evolving the Mobile Phone Orchestra." *NIME*, 2010, pp. 82-87.


### Smule / Ocarina — Ge Wang, Jeff Smith (2008-ongoing)

**Milieu / context:** Smule was founded in summer 2008 by Jeff Smith and Ge Wang — a direct commercialisation of MoPhO research. Ocarina was released late 2008, within months of the App Store's launch. It became one of Apple's all-time top 20 apps, downloaded by over 10 million users. By 2013, Smule had reached over 100 million users across all its apps. This is the moment where academic research in mobile music and Silicon Valley startup culture become indistinguishable.

**Intentions:** To create "a mobile musical experiment for the masses" (Wang 2014). Wang used "inside-out design" — starting from the iPhone's physical form factor and working backwards to find what instrument it naturally suggested. The iPhone was about the size of a four-hole ocarina, the microphone could serve as breath input, multi-touch could handle fingering, and accelerometers could add vibrato. A social dimension — the World Listener — would exploit GPS and persistent network connection. The stated ethos: "Sonic Media is based on the belief that everyone — not just 'musicians' and 'artists' — is capable of engaging with music" (Wang et al. 2009).

**Methods:** Ocarina used the iPhone's built-in sensors mapped to musical parameters: breath into the microphone controlled amplitude (via ChucK running on the device), multi-touch controlled pitch across 16 fingerings, accelerometers controlled vibrato (left-right for rate, front-back for depth). The synthesis was straightforward — a triangle wave modulated by a second oscillator for vibrato, multiplied against an amplitude envelope, fed through a reverberator. The visual design was rigorously physical — animated finger holes, breath gauge, no skeuomorphic "skin." Wang was emphatic: "the statement was not 'this simulates an ocarina,' but rather 'this *is* an ocarina.'"

The World Listener feature allowed anonymous listening: as a user played, an algorithm selected snippets for upload to a central server (no audio stored — only gesture data, re-synthesised on the listener's device). Listeners could hear strangers playing Ocarina anywhere on the globe, visualised on a spinning Earth — what Wang himself called "musical voyeurism."

Ocarina 2 (2012) added game-like elements: "breath points" as a measure of cumulative playing, achievements ("Every Breath You Take" at 300 breath points, "Lungevity" at 1,000,000), and a "songbook mode" that taught songs note-by-note where "this songbook mode follows the player, not the other way around."

**Conclusions:** Wang framed Ocarina's mass adoption — millions of downloads, thousands of YouTube videos, users playing everything from *Ode to Joy* to *Legend of Zelda* — as validation of the democratisation thesis. The CMJ paper reaches consistently for the language of magic and the sublime: "isn't hinting at magic a powerful way to hide technology, while encouraging users to focus on the experience?" The World Listener created moments where users could "forget the technology, and feel a small, yet nonetheless visceral, connection with strangers on the other side of the world." The paper closes with a review from a U.S. soldier deployed in Iraq: "This is my peace on earth... The globe feature that lets you hear everybody else in the world playing is the most calming art I have ever been introduced to. It brings the entire world together without politics or war."

Wang's own Discussion section reveals the tension at the heart of the project: "Is Ocarina itself a new form of art? Or is it a toy? Or maybe a bit of both? These are questions for each person to decide." He acknowledges "definite limitations to the mobile phone as a platform for crafting musical expression," noting that "traditional instruments are designed and crafted over decades or even centuries, whereas something like Ocarina was created in six weeks." But the concluding gesture is pure utopian reach: "The tendrils of possibility seem to reach out and plant the seeds for some yet-unknown global community. Is that not worth exploring?"

What goes unexamined: the fact that Ocarina was a commercial product distributed through Apple's walled garden, requiring a specific expensive device, with Apple taking a 30% cut; that the "inside-out design" started from the iPhone's affordances rather than from any musical need; that "non-musicians" playing pentatonic melodies into their phones is a very specific and limited form of "musical engagement"; that the World Listener — hearing anonymous strangers — is passive consumption dressed as participation; and that the game-like achievement system ("breath points") transposes the logic of engagement metrics directly into musical practice.

**Key papers:**
- Wang, G. "Designing Smule's Ocarina: The iPhone's Magic Flute." *NIME*, 2009, pp. 303-307.
- Wang, G. "Ocarina: Designing the iPhone's Magic Flute." *Computer Music Journal*, 38(2), 2014, pp. 8-21.
- Wang, G. et al. "SMULE = Sonic Media: An Intersection of the Mobile, Musical, and Social." *ICMC*, 2009.

## Proliferation (2010-2013)

### Mobile Music Workshops & Atau Tanaka (2004-2013)

**Milieu / context:** Tanaka (Sony CSL Paris, later Goldsmiths) was active in mobile music from 2004 — before the iPhone — working with PDAs and early sensor-equipped devices. He co-organised a series of mobile music workshops (Gothenburg 2004, Vancouver 2005, Brighton 2006) that established the field. His 2013 paper with Kazuhiro Jo and Adam Parkinson represents a rare critical turn.

**Intentions:** The 2013 *Organised Sound* paper, "Workshopping Participation in Music," set out to "problematise participation as a complex system of relations where agency and identity enter into play." The authors evoked Arnstein's (1969) *Ladder of Citizen Participation* — with its three levels of *non-participation*, *tokenism*, and *citizen power* — to examine what actually happens when people are invited into musical collaboration. They drew on Lessig's (2008) read/write culture and Small's (1998) *musicking*, proposing a shift from "Music One Plays" and "Music One Listens To" to a third category: "Music One Participates In."

**Methods:** The paper reported on four types of workshop conducted between 2009-2012:

1. **"How to Turn Your iPhone into a Musical Instrument"** — Half-day mobile music programming workshops (12-16 participants) using Pure Data, ad-hoc wireless comms, and sensor prototyping, culminating in a concert. Presented nine times across schools, festivals, and community groups.

2. **Chiptune Marching Band (CTMB)** — Participants built simple analogue sound-producing circuits on breadboards, powered by hand-crank generators, housed in personalised cardboard tubes. 2-3 hour workshops across seven different settings (artist-run centres, Maker Faires, art festivals, academic conferences).

3. **Aeolian Harp** — Participants built and performed a historical wind-driven instrument, learning acoustic theory through material engagement.

4. **Social Inclusion through the Digital Economy (SiDE)** — Workshops with marginalised young people (14-22 years old) in partnership with community organisations, aiming to show that "the very technologies of media consumption could become instruments of personal expression and everyday creativity."

**Conclusions:** This paper is the critical exception in the field. Key findings:

- **The paradox of the artist:** Drawing on Claire Bishop (2012) and Hal Foster (2004), they identified that participation in art is haunted by a paradox — "by embracing chance, by giving up control, by inviting others to participate in the production of the artwork... these pioneering figures faced a paradox from the very start: how to do away with art by making art." Foster noted a tendency toward "a promiscuity of collaboration" where "the discursivity and sociability in these works risks being illegible to the viewer," wondering if this "might reintroduce the artist as the principle figure and the primary exegete of the work."

- **iPhone workshops:** The facilitators' expertise and virtuosity *intimidated participants and actually hindered participation* in the final performances. "Only a few participants took up the challenge and invitation to perform." There was a "return of hierarchy despite all good intentions" — the facilitator, having established themselves as "sage on the stage," could not easily become "guide on the side."

- **CTMB:** With no performance plan, the marching "often devolved into a sociable walk by a group of people with strange cardboard tubes, and did not have the impact of a Happening." The paper observed that "musical performance does not come naturally or automatically, no matter how empowering the act of instrument building."

- **SiDE:** "Interestingly, these technologies were not always seized upon for their musical potential." In the RYWU workshop, young people from an economically depressed post-industrial area re-framed the workshop to produce a video game rather than music — "a key epiphany... was the realisation that advanced technology happened in their own backyard, and not just in far away places such as Japan or Silicon Valley."

- **The meta-designer:** The paper concluded that "the goal is not to make musicians out of workshop participants" and that the artist becomes a "meta-designer" (Giaccardi 2005) — "intervening in perspectives of sound-making practice by sculpting levels of participation. The resulting representation is a collaborative one that is empowering for both author and participant, but is not a flat hierarchy, and can preserve defined, albeit non-traditional roles."

**Key papers:**
- Jo, K., Parkinson, A. and Tanaka, A. "Workshopping Participation in Music." *Organised Sound*, 18(3), 2013, pp. 282-291.
- Gaye, L. et al. "Mobile Music Technology: Report on an Emerging Community." *NIME*, 2006, pp. 22-25.
- Tanaka, A. "Mapping Out Instruments, Affordances, and Mobiles." *NIME*, 2010.


### massMobile — Jason Freeman, Nathan Weitzner et al. (2012-2015)

**Milieu / context:** Georgia Tech, Center for Music Technology. Freeman had been working on audience participation systems since *Glimmer* (2005, light sticks and cameras) and *Flock* (2008, simple analogue interfaces influencing onstage music). massMobile was the smartphone-era evolution — leveraging devices audiences already carried. The 2012 NIME paper explicitly acknowledged: "as the number of participants grows, it becomes increasingly challenging to maintain a balance between the transparency of individual contributions and the coherency of the collective product."

**Intentions:** To create "a client-server system for mass audience participation in live performances using smartphones" enabling "real-time bi-directional communication between performers and audiences utilizing existing wireless 3G, 4G, or WiFi networks." Freeman framed this as creating "a sense of shared community among audience members and a dynamic of risk, spontaneity, excitement and uniqueness at live concerts" — explicitly pushing against "the perfection of studio recordings through a reliance on prerecorded content and intensely pre-choreographed musical expressivity" (Freeman et al. 2015).

**Methods:** Client-server architecture. Audience members accessed a browser-based web app via short URL or QR code. The interface supported sliders, text entry, voting, drawing, and a 2-D slider for simultaneous x/y input. Data was sent to a cloud-based server (originally Java/MySQL, later Node.js/MongoDB), polled by a Max/MSP client onstage that drove the performance. massMobile was deliberately designed as a *framework* rather than a single piece — different compositions could deploy different interfaces.

Two major deployments were documented in the 2015 *Digital Creativity* paper:

1. **TeamWork** (2013) — Collaboration with the Georgia Tech Pep Band at basketball games. Fans used a 2-D slider (slow/fast on x-axis, soft/loud on y-axis) to influence the band's tempo and dynamics during halftime performances. Four events at McCamish Pavilion, official attendance ranging from 624 to 7,128. The scoreboard displayed aggregate audience input.

2. **Saxophone Etudes** (2012) — A 10-minute composition for solo saxophone and audience. Audience members voted on which notes or melodic fragments the saxophonist should play. Two concerts, 45 total participants.

**Conclusions:** The massMobile papers contain some of the most revealing empirical data in this entire literature, and it substantially undermines the democratisation thesis:

**TeamWork findings:**
- Participation rates were dismal: **1.1% to 3%** of stadium attendance actually used the system (Table 1 in the 2015 paper). At the largest event (6,962 fans), only 19 people participated.
- At the first large performance (Feb 3, 108 fans participating): "it averaged out, so nothing happened." The aggregate of all votes converged on the centre — moderate tempo, moderate dynamics. Fans complained that "they need better feedback as to how my participation affected the performance, if at all."
- When individual votes were displayed, a leader-follower dynamic emerged: "a group dynamic quickly developed in which 'leaders' would change their votes and then 'followers' would gradually shift over to one leader, collectively effecting a change." Emergent hierarchy reasserted itself within the supposedly flat participatory structure.
- The researchers resorted to *exaggerating* small deviations from the average on the scoreboard to make fans feel their input mattered — "this method exaggerated even small deviations from the exact center of the screen while keeping the interpretation meaningful."
- They also invited the team mascot to run around the court with signs reading "loud," "soft," "fast," "slow" to guide voting — reintroducing precisely the centralised authority the system was designed to eliminate.

**Saxophone Etudes findings:**
- Despite "active participation" (6,954 votes across 45 participants, averaging 155 per person), **62.5% of participants disagreed** that their actions influenced other audience members. When asked if they influenced the saxophonist, **50% were neutral**.
- "Many audience members struggled to learn the interface itself or to trace their activities through to the saxophonist, given the short timespan of the performance."
- The saxophonist "expressed continual frustration that the audience activity meter did not change more dramatically over the course of the performance."
- 80% of votes were logged during the first movement, only 20% during the second — engagement dropped sharply.

Freeman's own conclusion gestured toward the problem without naming it structurally: "Network design is usually constrained by the large number of simultaneous inputs from audience participants and the primacy of a single musical result that all participants hear" (2015). The solution proposed was always better interface design, better feedback, better mappings — never a questioning of whether the promise of meaningful individual expression at scale was structurally possible.

**Key papers:**
- Weitzner, N., Freeman, J., Garrett, S. and Chen, Y.-L. "massMobile — an Audience Participation Framework." *NIME*, 2012.
- Weitzner, N., Freeman, J. et al. "massMobile: towards a flexible framework for large-scale participatory collaborations in live performances." *Organised Sound*, 18(1), 2013, pp. 30-42.
- Freeman, J. et al. "Using massMobile, a flexible, scalable, rapid prototyping audience participation framework, in large-scale live musical performances." *Digital Creativity*, 26(3-4), 2015, pp. 228-244.


### NEXUS — Jesse Allison, Yemin Oh, Benjamin Taylor (2013)

**Milieu / context:** Louisiana State University. Allison had been working on browser-based distribution since 2011 (NIME tutorial on Ruby on Rails and HTML5). The Web Audio API had landed in Chrome in 2011-2012, making browser-based synthesis viable without plugins. This was a pivotal technical moment — instruments could be distributed via URL rather than app store.

**Intentions:** "Collaborative Performance for the Masses, Handling Instrument Interface Distribution through the Web." The explicit claim: interfaces "can reach potential performers by distributing a unique user interface to any device with a browser anywhere in the world." This is the universalist promise at its most expansive — no app store, no download, just a URL.

**Methods:** Web-based (HTML5, Web Audio API). Instrument interfaces served to participant devices via browser. Each participant received a "unique user interface" — some degree of role differentiation rather than everyone getting the same controls.

**Conclusions:** The browser-based approach is technically closest to Thomas Capogreco's own practice. The critical question — unanswered in Allison's paper — is whether "the masses" actually produce meaningful musical outcomes with these interfaces, or whether distribution was treated as the achievement in itself. The title alone — "Collaborative Performance for the Masses" — reveals the framing.

**Key papers:**
- Allison, J., Oh, Y. and Taylor, B. "NEXUS: Collaborative Performance for the Masses, Handling Instrument Interface Distribution through the Web." *NIME*, 2013.


### echobo — Sang Won Lee & Jason Freeman (2013)

**Milieu / context:** Collaboration between University of Michigan (where Essl had established the Michigan Mobile Phone Ensemble) and Georgia Tech. Part of a cluster exploring "large-scale audience participation using mobile phones as musical instruments."

**Intentions:** To leverage "the ubiquity of smartphones to accomplish audience engagement" by transforming "the audience's smartphones into instruments used to accompany the performer" (Freeman et al. 2015).

**Methods:** Audience devices became sound sources — closer to the distributed synthesis model than the voting/control model of massMobile.

**Conclusions:** Lee went on to explore live coding for audience participation (ICLC 2016) — moving from GUI-based participation toward code-based, suggesting dissatisfaction with the simplicity of touchscreen interfaces.

**Key papers:**
- Lee, S.W. and Freeman, J. "echobo: Audience Participation Using The Mobile Music Instrument." *NIME*, 2013.

## Spectacle & Late-Period Projects (2011-2016)

### DigiEnsemble Berlin (2011-ongoing)

**Milieu / context:** Berlin. Not an academic research project but a performance ensemble — "the first genuine band performing on mobile devices only." Ten professional musicians playing repertoire from Bach to metal using iPhone, iPad, and commercial apps.

**Intentions:** Spectacle-driven: classical music "liberated" from traditional instruments by consumer devices.

**Methods:** Professional musicians using commercial apps (SampleTank, Beatmaker2, iDensity) in rehearsed ensemble performance. No custom software, no participatory element.

**Conclusions:** Significant as a cultural symptom rather than a research contribution. The fact that it required professional musicians to produce convincing results quietly contradicted the democratisation narrative — these were not "the masses" making music, but trained virtuosi transplanting their skills onto consumer hardware.


### Rottondi et al. — Overview Paper (2016)

**Milieu / context:** An overview paper in *IEEE Access* suggesting the field had matured enough to warrant consolidation.

**Key papers:**
- Rottondi, C., Chafe, C., Allocchio, C. and Sarti, A. "An Overview on Networked Music Performance Technologies." *IEEE Access*, 2016.

## Critical Perspectives (rare)

### Victoria Simon — "Guided by Delight" (2018/2020)

**Milieu / context:** Published in *Television & New Media*. Simon examined music apps (ThumbJam, iMaschine 2, Skram) as products of iOS platform design conventions.

**Intentions:** To argue that music apps "promise to broaden musical participation through simple, user-friendly interface design," but that within dominant conventions, "'fun' is synonymous with instant success and effortless musical mastery."

**Conclusions:** Simon identified a structural tension: the design logic of iOS (frictionless, delightful, instantly rewarding) is fundamentally at odds with the demands of meaningful musical practice (which requires friction, difficulty, and sustained engagement). The platform's UX ideology shapes the instruments built on it.

**Key papers:**
- Simon, V. "Guided by Delight: Music Apps and the Politics of User Interface Design in the iOS Platform." *Television & New Media*, 2020.

## Summary: The Rhetorical Landscape

The dominant rhetoric across this body of work clusters around several claims:

1. **Democratisation / universalism**: "everyone" can make music; the smartphone makes musical expression available to "the masses" (Wang, Allison, Freeman). Mobile phones "transcend nearly every cultural and economic barrier" (Wang 2008).

2. **Magical connectivity**: technology creates "magical" global social bonds through music (Wang's Ocarina papers especially). "Tendrils of possibility seem to reach out and plant the seeds for some yet-unknown global community" (Wang 2014).

3. **Barrier-breaking**: these tools "blur the lines between composition, performance and listening" (Freeman 2015), "redistributing the roles of composer, performer, and listener."

4. **The neutral platform assumption**: virtually none of these papers interrogate Apple's platform politics, the App Store's gatekeeping, the requirement to own a specific expensive device, or the broader political economy of consumer electronics. The iPhone is treated as transparent infrastructure.

5. **Interaction for interaction's sake**: participation is framed as inherently valuable. The question is always *how* to enable more participation, never *whether* participation at scale produces meaningful expression.

The **empirical findings**, however, tell a different story:

- **massMobile/TeamWork**: 1-3% participation rates; aggregate inputs "averaged out, so nothing happened"; leader-follower hierarchies spontaneously re-emerged; researchers resorted to exaggerating data and deploying mascots.
- **massMobile/Saxophone Etudes**: 62.5% of participants couldn't perceive their influence on others; the performer was frustrated by the lack of visible audience effect.
- **Jo, Parkinson & Tanaka workshops**: facilitator virtuosity intimidated and hindered participation; marching performances devolved into aimless walks; participants in social inclusion workshops rejected the musical framing entirely.

The **critical exceptions** are notable for their rarity:
- **Jo, Parkinson & Tanaka (2013)**: identified "possible contradictions in horizontal utopian organisational models"; concluded that participation "is not a flat hierarchy."
- **Hajdu**: honestly acknowledged latency as a constitutive constraint.
- **Simon (2018/2020)**: directly critiqued iOS platform design ideology.

The vast majority of the field operated within what might accurately be described as complicity with the techno-utopian logic of consumer technology — excited by the iPhone's promise without critically examining the platform politics or the structural impossibility of the democratic claim.
